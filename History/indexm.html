History of Ethiopia

100 AD

Kingdom of Aksum

The Aksumite Dynasty was a powerful ancient civilization that flourished in present-day Ethiopia and Eritrea between the 1st and 7th centuries CE. It was one of the great African empires, known for its advanced architecture, including massive obelisks, and its early adoption of Christianity in the 4th century under King Ezana. Aksum was a major trade hub, linking Africa with the Mediterranean, Arabia, and India. The kingdom developed its own script, Ge'ez, and minted coins, showcasing its economic strength. Despite its decline due to environmental and political factors, Aksum’s legacy remains central to Ethiopian identity and Orthodox Christianity.

1270 AD

Solomonic Dynasty

The Solomonic Dynasty ruled Ethiopia from 1270 to 1974, claiming descent from the biblical King Solomon and the Queen of Sheba. Founded by Emperor Yekuno Amlak, it restored Ethiopian rule after the Zagwe Dynasty. The dynasty strengthened Orthodox Christianity, expanded the empire, and resisted foreign invasions, including the Ottoman and Italian forces. Prominent rulers like Emperor Menelik II modernized Ethiopia and defeated Italy at the Battle of Adwa in 1896. Emperor Haile Selassie, the last Solomonic ruler, introduced reforms but was overthrown in 1974. The dynasty left a lasting legacy in Ethiopian culture, religion, and national identity.

1529 - 1543

Adal-Ethiopian Wars

The Adal-Ethiopian Wars (1529–1543) were a series of fierce battles between the Ethiopian Empire and the Muslim Sultanate of Adal, led by Imam Ahmed Ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi, also known as Ahmed Gragn ("the Left-Handed"). The conflict began when Adal launched a jihad to expand Islamic rule, devastating Ethiopia with superior firearms acquired from the Ottomans. Ethiopian Emperor Lebna Dengel struggled to resist until Portugal intervened in 1541, sending musketeers to aid Ethiopia. After years of destruction, Ahmed Gragn was killed in battle in 1543, leading to Adal’s decline. The war reshaped Ethiopian history, weakening both sides and inviting future foreign influences.

1889

Reign of Emperor Menelik II

Emperor Menelik II (1889–1913) was a transformative ruler of Ethiopia, known for modernizing the nation and defending its sovereignty. His greatest achievement was the victory at the Battle of Adwa in 1896, where Ethiopian forces decisively defeated the Italian army, making Ethiopia the only African nation to resist European colonization successfully. Menelik expanded Ethiopia’s territory, established Addis Ababa as the capital, and introduced modern infrastructure, including roads, telegraphs, and a railway. He also initiated administrative reforms and strengthened diplomatic ties with global powers. Despite health issues later in life, his reign laid the foundation for a strong, independent Ethiopia.

1935 - 1941

Italian Occupation

The Italian occupation of Ethiopia (1936–1941) was a five-year period during which Fascist Italy, under Benito Mussolini, invaded and briefly controlled Ethiopia. After a brutal campaign using chemical weapons and aerial bombings, Italy annexed Ethiopia into its East African Empire. Emperor Haile Selassie fled into exile and appealed to the League of Nations, but international support was limited. Ethiopian patriots (Arbegnoch) waged fierce resistance, disrupting Italian rule. In 1941, with the support of British and Commonwealth forces, Ethiopia was liberated, and Haile Selassie was restored to the throne. The occupation strengthened Ethiopian nationalism and resistance against colonial aggression.

1974

End of Monarchy

The Ethiopian monarchy ended in 1974 when Emperor Haile Selassie was overthrown by the Derg, a Marxist-Leninist military junta led by Mengistu Haile Mariam. Economic struggles, corruption, famine, and rising public dissatisfaction fueled the revolution. Students, workers, and the military joined forces to depose the emperor, marking the end of a monarchy that had lasted for centuries. Haile Selassie was placed under house arrest and later died under suspicious circumstances in 1975. The Derg abolished the monarchy, declared Ethiopia a socialist state, and ruled with an iron fist, leading to years of political turmoil, civil war, and human rights abuses.

1991

Fall of the Derg

The Derg regime fell in 1991 after years of civil war, economic decline, and internal dissent. Led by Mengistu Haile Mariam, the Derg ruled Ethiopia with an authoritarian socialist system, implementing land reforms but also engaging in brutal purges and mass killings known as the Red Terror. Armed opposition groups, particularly the Ethiopian People's Revolutionary Democratic Front (EPRDF) and Eritrean separatists, intensified their resistance throughout the 1980s. With Soviet support dwindling, the Derg weakened, and in May 1991, the EPRDF forces entered Addis Ababa, forcing Mengistu to flee to Zimbabwe. The fall of the Derg led to Ethiopia's transition to a federal system.
